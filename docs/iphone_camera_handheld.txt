The thing to internalize first: shake hurts you through two completely separate mechanisms, and the camera can only fix one of them.
Motion blur within the exposure smears every glyph edge while the shutter is open — that's the fatal one for per-tile classification, and it's what "even tiny shakes" is doing to you. Inter-frame displacement (the field sitting somewhere new each frame) is the other, and that one is a geometry problem, not a camera problem — it's what your Handheld toggle (per-frame re-lock on CRC fail) and RESEARCH_FAILS exist to absorb. No camera setting fixes geometry drift; keep leaning on the re-search for that.
So the camera side is really about killing blur. And here the good news is that your single best lever has zero side effects on your homography pipeline:
Short exposure is the whole game, and #8 is tuned far too gently for handheld. Blur is linear in exposure time. Your auto-picked ~25 ms at a fixed rig is already marginal; #8 only takes you to 1/125 (8 ms) and it's off by default. For handheld, push it to 1/500 or 1/1000. At 1 ms, even tremor-level angular velocity (~10–20°/s) smears sub-pixel — hand shake is simply frozen. The cost is ISO: 1/40→1/1000 is ~4.6 stops, ISO ~90→~2000+, which is fine because your own classifier notes say moderate sensor noise is survivable and blur is not. Brighten the panel a notch to buy some of that back. Critically, this touches nothing in your geometry — the field stays a clean planar projective image, homography fit unaffected.
The OIS trap — and it's a real trap. Your instinct might be "turn on optical stabilization, it fights shake in hardware." But on iPhone, video OIS is not free or always-on. Per Apple's own guidance, the rear camera is optically stabilized for video only when you set preferredVideoStabilizationMode to something other than .off — the property automatically enables both optical and digital stabilization on devices supporting video OIS. That means OIS comes bundled with EIS: frame warp, ~10–20% FoV crop, and non-rigid rolling-shutter correction — exactly the rubber-banding that breaks a projective homography. Your #7 can only ever set .off, so today you're running with neither OIS nor EIS (clean, but blur-prone). Getting OIS requires accepting EIS warp on top, and even with per-frame re-search the non-rigid component may fight your four-corner fit. It's testable (add a mode that sets .standard, the lightest; never .cinematic), but it's an experiment, not a win — and if your exposure is short enough, you don't need OIS at all. I'd exhaust the exposure axis before touching the stabilization axis.
Rolling-shutter jello: turn OFF 4K (#9) for handheld. Camera motion during sensor readout shears the field non-projectively — the diagonal-stripe artifact, now driven by your hand instead of just the panel refresh. Nothing eliminates it, but it scales with readout time, and 4K roughly doubles readout versus 1080p binned. So handheld inverts your "4K for r12 sharpness" logic: 1080p binned has the shortest readout and the least jello. Keep #1+#2, drop #9.
60 fps + FA best-shot is your real handheld ally. Hands have micro-still-points — brief moments where angular velocity crosses zero. More shots per dwell means more chances one lands in that pause, where both blur and jello go to ~0. Camera-side you already have this (#1+#2 in production); the leverage is on the receiver picking the sharpest of the 2–3 shots per dwell rather than best-effort dedup. That's FA_ANALYZE_FRAMES / FA_ENABLED_IN_TRAINING territory.
One failure mode handheld introduces that a fixed rig never had: focus Z-wobble. You lock focus in settleAndLock, but at f/1.78, ~40–60 cm, the depth of field is only a few cm. A handheld drift of several cm toward or away from the screen softens glyphs even with zero angular shake and zero blur. Bracing your elbows or resting the phone against something fixes the Z-drift far more than it fixes the angular shake — which is the usual reason "handheld but braced" decodes and "handheld freehand" doesn't.
Priority order if you're A/B-testing through the Settings toggles: crank #8 shorter (biggest, cleanest), confirm #9 off, keep 60 fps, lean on FA picking, brace for Z. Only after all that, if blur still shows up, consider the OIS-via-EIS experiment — and expect to earn it back in geometry residual.
